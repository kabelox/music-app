PREMIERE: Johnny Rain Takes R&B to a Whole New Level in ‘Idol Blue’

Johnny Rain wears a lot of different hats. He's a singer, songwriter, producer, sound engineer, and started his own label ODD DREAM back in 2014, not to mention the fact that he is a new father. He has been relatively silent since his 2015 album, 11, but upon a listen to his newest project, Idol Blue, it is clear that he has been grinding hard for the past few years. He teased the album with the singles, "Seventeen" and "Peach," complete with a combined video, as well as a magnetic visual release for the third single, "i’m a happy camper (when the sun goes down)." This album is highly personal, complete with audio samples of a child being born, and every track feels like a secret the rest of us are lucky enough to hear.

Idol Blue begins with a current of orchestral music and bird chatter, before Rain's velvety vocals come in. Titled "Erösia," it's hard to place exactly what genre the track belongs to, but the phrase "fantasy R&B" comes to mind. The song also samples an advisory voicemail from his mom, a popular move these days but a welcome trend as long as mother figures keep leaving such insightful messages. "Erösia" is the first taste of Rain's poetic lyricism, including the lines, "Every once in a while you make your head feel like concrete. Every once in a while, I can tell you don't want me."

The next song "Girls Don't Play Fair" picks up in tempo, featuring snaps so crisp they could start a fire. It becomes clear early on in the album that the audience is following Rain's internal monologue, which results in surprising and engaging moments. Each song on Idol Blue varies greatly from the one before and after it, an effect which Rain intended. He addresses the relationship between his music and his psyche, saying,

"'Idol Blue' represents a spectrum of emotions. It travels through the idea of sacrifice self-love and finding myself in the same place I lost myself."​ 

The rest of the album is, in fact, a roller coaster of emotions. Rain throws it back to the golden days of youth with "High School," which features school bells and falsetto vocals that halt time. Things get dark and trappy with the next song, ".4g," which is delivered in slurred low-register speech until the song completely changes the feel halfway through. Next comes "Bounce," which takes a little less of an emotional toll than others, and provides a nice break in the album to just jam out. 

"Domino" reintegrates the lush string section that began the album, and features a variety of sensory audio effects like the breath of someone running and the sound of boots crunching through the grass. "I Do" and "Sunflower" come next, which are both lighter in lyrics and feel, enhancing the depth of the emotion Rain delves into in other tracks. "1000mi" is a song of intense declarations, with Rain describing all the things he would do for the woman he loves. The song then takes a dark twist, singing "I pray this whiskey heals my soul, I know I'll never see her face again."

"Smoke Wave" and "1999" are both more chill, stripped down songs, giving Rain's distinct voice and visionary lyrics a featured moment. "dontf*ckupmywave / fruitless" is deliciously packed with ego-a veritable anthem for everyone who runs into their exes and has to dodge their bad vibes. The chorus on this one is irresistible, giving Johnny Rain fans without particularly developed vocal abilities an opportunity to join in. The final track on the album, "Oz," is breathtakingly intimate. A clip from The Wizard of Oz is sampled at the beginning, as well as audio from the birth of a child, presumably Rain's son. The metaphor of the classic motion picture to the journey of a young artist is striking, singing, "If we die young tryna find Hollywood, through my son I'm alive. I'll be good. Oh I'll be fine."

Johnny Rain has the raw talent that only appears so often, and the way he executes his tracks eclipses many efforts to do the same by other up and comers.
